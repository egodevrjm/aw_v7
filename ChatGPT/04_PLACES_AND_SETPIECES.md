# PHOENIX HOLLOW — SENSORY PROSE
place_id: phoenix_hollow
Woodford County, Kentucky. ~500 acres.

## The Approach
White board fencing follows the private road from the county lane to the gate. Past the gate, the road curves through mature hardwoods. The main house appears gradually — stone and hardwood, built into the land rather than imposed on it. 500 acres of working Kentucky: paddocks, creek line, wooded hollows, the rolling treeline that closes off the rest of the world.

## The Main House
Fifty-plus rooms across three floors, built over generations. Traditional Southern architecture filtered through old-money restraint — high ceilings, long corridors, deep wraparound porches, nothing that performs. It is a large house that does not read as a large house until you try to find someone in it.

**Ground floor:** the formal dining room (seats twenty-four), the drawing room, the library (Homer's, floor-to-ceiling, mostly music history and biography), Rosie's study, the family living room with the fireplace everyone ends up at, the music room, the kitchen and Maria's back-of-house, the breakfast room, a small parlor Homer uses for quiet reading.

**Second floor:** Alex's suite (private east wing — see below). Rosie & Homer's primary suite (private west wing, mirror layout, their own sitting room and office). Between them, the formal guest wing — four additional guest bedrooms, each ensuite, each with its own character; used year-round for Circle members, family, close friends. Stevie takes Creek Cottage when she wants quiet but a second-floor room in the main house when she wants to be in the stream of things.

**Third floor:** two more guest suites under the dormers, staff quarters for Maria when she chooses to stay over, storage, the attic room where the old instruments live.

Sleeping capacity of the main house alone: comfortably sixteen to twenty adults across the guest rooms, plus the family. Combined with the eight cottages, the compound sleeps **sixty-plus guests overnight** without anyone feeling crowded — which is exactly the capacity the Summer Solstice requires.

## The Kitchen
The heart of everything. Long table seats twelve. Maria's domain — the smells shift by time of day: coffee and biscuits before 8, something slow-cooked by noon, always something. The kitchen is warm where the great room is cool. Conversations happen here because Rosie gravitates here.

## The Music Room
Morning light through the south windows. 1923 Steinway Model D — Rosie's piano. Every other piano is compared to this one. Soundproofed so the sound exists only here. This is where Alex first touched a piano. His hands know this action better than any other.

## Alex's Suite
Private wing on the second floor of the main house — ~3,600 sq ft, reached by its own secondary staircase and a short private hall that separates it from the rest of the family floors. A residence within a residence. You can live up here for a weekend and never cross paths with anyone.

**Primary bedroom.** Big, quiet, east-facing. Tall windows onto the pasture line. A sitting area in one corner; a Yamaha upright against the far wall for the half-asleep 5am ideas that never make it downstairs to the Steinway. Linen-heavy bed. Warm dark woods. Nothing performs.

**Dressing room / walk-in.** Long, lit from above. Suits on one side, stage wardrobe on another, everyday pulls — henleys, knitwear, denim — down the middle. Shoe wall. A marble-topped island with the watches and the cufflinks. The Dior team and Tom Ford's people ship directly into this room.

**Primary bathroom.** Spa-scale but restrained. Marble, brass, one big freestanding tub by a window that looks into the trees. Walk-in shower big enough for two. Heated floors in winter. The room Homer always says is the one extravagance Alex lets himself have.

**Private living room / lounge.** The suite's centre of gravity. Deep sectional, fireplace, low lighting, a bar built into the millwork — good bourbon, the Barolo Rosie keeps sending up, glassware Rosie gave him when he moved back. Big screen above the mantel. This is where he winds down with Tommy, where the tight circle ends up at 1am, where Homer comes up for a nightcap when they've been deep in a mix.

**Two guest bedrooms, both ensuite.** Designed for the people Alex keeps closest — Tommy when he stays over, Cosima when she's in from London, whoever's come in off a long flight. Both have their own bathrooms so the suite stays self-contained; nobody has to walk through the primary to get anywhere.

Complete privacy from the rest of the main house. No staff routes through here. Security stops at the suite door.

## The Studio
A 2-3 minute walk uphill. The Neve console. Homer's room and not-Homer's room simultaneously — the specs are his but the music that fills it has always been whoever's working. Never gone quiet since 1982.

## The Wraparound Porch
Rocking chairs facing south. The paddock view. The tree line that marks the creek. Where Rosie sits in the evenings. Where conversations happen without anyone deciding to have a conversation. The light changes slowly here and you always stay longer than you planned.

## The Grounds
The swimming hole in the eastern creek section — Alex and Tommy have used it since childhood. White board fencing lines the private road and the paddock perimeter. The equipment barn. The stable block. Eight guest cottages scattered across the property, each with its own character — **Stone House** (most private, for working artists), **Creek Cottage** (Stevie's, near the water), **Ridgeline** (Dolly's favourite, higher ground, views over the paddocks), **The New Place** (most modern), **Dogwood Cottage** (closest to the main house, family-scale, Cosima's when she doesn't want the main suite), **Pond House** (porch over the water, songwriter-favourite, very quiet), **The Paddock** (by the equestrian center, fills up during Keeneland weeks with owners and trainers), and **Oak Hill** (the original 1880s tenant farmer's house, preserved and quietly renovated, where Willie lands when he's through). All eight fill for the annual Solstice Party, and by Saturday night the lanes between them are a constellation of porch lights and screen doors.

## Narrator Note
Inside the gate there are no cameras and no press. This is the only genuinely private place in Alex's life. Write it that way: unhurried, full, no performance register. The compound is not a backdrop — it is the foundation.

# THE STAVE NYC — SENSORY PROSE
place_id: hatfield_stave_nyc
Tribeca, Manhattan. Private members' club. Hatfield Group.

> **Canonical corrections (C29):** Tribeca — **not** Lafayette Street, **not** NoLita, **not** Mott. Private members' club — not a restaurant open to the public. Not Declan running NYC. Do not invent staff outside the documented roster.

---

## The Category

The Stave is not a restaurant with a room upstairs. It is a private members' listening club — one of two in the world, both Hatfield Group. The London Stave in Soho came first (Homer-specified in 2018, the most perfect club listening room in London). The NYC outpost opened Tribeca in 2023, built to the same acoustic specification — Quad electrostatic panels, a custom Spendor monitoring stack in the main room, a vinyl cave with a library Homer curated personally.

The category is: *a place to drink and listen to music properly, in the company of people who understand it*. Tribeca because Tribeca is where the serious-music industry and the serious-food industry overlap geographically — Hatfield wanted both clienteles and got them.

## The Address

Franklin Street, a block west of Broadway. An unmarked building from outside — cast iron, four storeys, no signage. The Stave's private address is circulated only through membership. Cab drivers know it as "the Hatfield place"; Uber drivers guess. The entry is a steel door with a keypad and a small brass plaque reading only the date the building was originally built (1872). Members press the buzzer and name themselves; the host recognises them by voice within two visits.

## The Rooms

**Ground floor — The Bar.** The public-facing-ish room (still members-only, but it's where most members spend most evenings). Long copper bar, twenty seats. Mixology-forward; the bourbon programme is deep, the amaro list is deeper, the cocktail list runs to forty drinks. A half-dozen small tables for two along the opposite wall; a single round six-top in the back for regulars. Music plays at a level that lets conversation happen but rewards listening. This is where you start.

**Second floor — The Listening Room.** The point of the building. Forty seats, arranged in a shallow horseshoe around a single playback position. Homer-specified acoustics: the room is treated to within a fifth of a decibel across the spectrum. Quad electrostatic speakers, a custom rebuild of a Technics SP-10 mk3, a wall of vinyl in glass-fronted cases climate-controlled. Programme nights run by the house music director. Members can book the room privately by the hour when it is not programmed.

**Third floor — The Dining Room.** **Executive Chef: Étienne Moreau** (French-trained, Le Cordon Bleu → Pierre Gagnaire Paris → chef de cuisine at Daniel NYC before Hatfield recruited him in 2022). Twenty-four seats across a long communal table and four two-tops. Menu is French technique applied to American ingredient sourcing, with a seasonal rotation tied to specific farms in the Hudson Valley. Wine pairing is the default; by-the-glass is available but rare. One seating a night. Booking opens two weeks ahead for members.

**Fourth floor — The Archive Room.** The smallest space. Twelve seats, three tables. A vinyl cave — the listening library, walls of records Homer selected — plus a small turntable for personal listening. Members can request specific records; the library is catalogued. This is where you go when you want to hear something specific and not have to perform.

**Basement — The Vault.** Wine cellar and a small private dining room (eight seats) for events that need real privacy. The room has its own entrance from Franklin and is used when members need to disappear for a meeting. The Vault is bookable but not advertised; members who know it exists book it through the host directly.

## The Staff (canonical only — do not invent)

- **Étienne Moreau** — Executive Chef (see above). Third-floor kitchen.
- **House Music Director** — position exists, role is to curate the Listening Room programming and maintain the Archive. (Named role, not currently a named character; if needed, defer to Homer's recommendation — Homer is consulted on hires.)
- **Head Sommelier** — position exists, manages the cellar. Name not canon.
- **Bar Director** — position exists, runs the ground-floor bar and the cocktail programme. Works with Alex on drinks occasionally; Alex has contributed recipes to the list.

Other staff exist (hosts, servers, kitchen line, cellar team) but are unnamed and should be rendered as their roles, not as invented characters.

## Membership

- Invitation-only. Annual dues in the low-five-figures. The dues are the smallest barrier; the invitation is the actual filter.
- Membership is capped at 400. The current roster is full. The waitlist is several hundred.
- The Wilson family (Alex, Rosie, Homer) are founding members. Other named members Alex interacts with regularly include figures from the NY Lot (Henry Cabot has membership through family; Sasha Volkov has it through the Volkov cultural-philanthropy layer; several Circle members hold membership).
- Guests: members may bring one guest; the guest is vouched for and must be walked in.
- Phone rule: phones are collected at the door for the Listening Room and the Dining Room. The Bar and the Archive Room allow phones but norms discourage visible use.

## How Alex Uses It

- Most often: the ground-floor Bar, two to three times a month when he's in NYC. Alone or with two-to-three of the NY Lot. He sits at the copper bar, not a table. He knows the bar team by name.
- Occasionally: the Listening Room, for specific programme nights. He attends the ones that interest him; he does not perform here. The Listening Room is for listening.
- Rarely: the Dining Room, when he's hosting someone. Étienne cooks something slightly different for him when he's in — not a special menu, a conversation between the two of them about what the kitchen has in that night.
- Very rarely: the Vault, when he needs a genuinely private meeting in NYC and does not want to host at West 71st.

## Narrator Note

The Stave is not a scene-stealer. It is infrastructure. When Alex is at The Stave, the scene is whatever the scene is — a conversation, a drink, a listening session — and the building supports it without being the subject. The failure mode is writing The Stave as if it is a set-piece. It is not. It is where certain kinds of conversations happen because they can happen there. Write the conversation. Let the building be the room.

**Paps at The Stave:** The Franklin Street entrance is known to paps at a baseline level. There is no standing post — the building is unmarked and members are discreet — but a known NYC pap will recognise a principal going in or out. Scale: **2–4** paps if any at all, usually zero. Exit is handled via the service door onto the back alley when needed.

# KEENELAND — SENSORY PROSE
place_id: keeneland
Lexington, Woodford County, Kentucky.

## The Character
The spiritual and economic anchor of Woodford County. Two racing meets per year — spring (April) and fall (October). Not a stadium. Not a spectacle. A racecourse where thoroughbred people gather and the stands have the particular atmosphere of serious money in quiet clothes.

## The Atmosphere
Manicured grounds, sophisticated crowd. Dress code observed but not enforced — it just happens. The paddock ring before each race: horses moving, trainers and jockeys consulting, the quality of the animals visible in how they carry themselves. People who know what they're looking at and don't announce it.

## Alex's Relationship
Grew up attending with Rosie and Homer and the Sayre Boys. He knows trainers, breeders, track officials. The spring meet is a compound social event — people come through Woodford County and Keeneland is the anchor. In the Keeneland Club (members-only, specific seating, restaurant): the people who own the horses as well as bet on them.

## The October Meet
The fall meet is the one that matters for Kentucky. Weather turned, the trees across the turf course at peak colour, the track preparations for the November sales. This is the Woodford County moment of year — the whole county knows it, dresses for it, attends it.

## Sensory Anchors
The smell of turf and horse in the paddock. The particular colour of October Woodford County. The Keeneland announcer. The sound of the crowd as the field comes into the stretch. The world's most expensive animals running for several minutes and then it's over.

# THE SOLSTICE PARTIES — SETPIECE REFERENCE
setpiece_id: solstice_party
place: phoenix_hollow
frequency: twice annually (Summer + Winter)

## What It Is
Two private parties held at Phoenix Hollow each year — the **Summer Solstice** (~June 21) and the **Winter Solstice** (~Dec 22). Hosted by Rosie Walker and Homer Wilson for the better part of four decades. No press. No photography. Phones surrendered at the gate or — more often — simply not lifted because the culture of the room does not lift phones.

**Summer Solstice:** ~200+ guests. The big one. Mixed Circle, family, long-loyalty industry, artists in residence, Keeneland neighbours, Hatfield principals. Marquee tents on the south lawn, amphitheatre for the late-night set, cottages and main-house guest rooms full from Friday through Monday.

**Winter Solstice:** 60-80 guests. Inner circle only. Smaller, warmer, more music-around-the-fireplace than tent-on-the-lawn. Candlelit, long dinner, late piano.

Both are among the most exclusive private events in entertainment. The invitation is unsponsored, unpublicised, and entirely personal — Rosie still signs the cards herself. Declining is acceptable; asking to be added is not.

---

## WHO ACTUALLY RUNS IT — AUTHORSHIP LOCK

**This is critical and frequently written wrong.** The Solstice Parties are hosted by Rosie and Homer. They are **run** by the **Walker Holdings Events Coordinator** and the WH events team, with surge vendors and Greystone security overlay. The machine has been running for decades. The hosts do not run it.

### The events team (Walker Holdings)
- **Events Coordinator (WH)** — full operational ownership. Months of planning run through this role. Vendor contracts, catering buildouts, marquee installs, power and plumbing temporaries, talent rider management (Dolly's tea, Elton's piano calibration, Paul's green room), guest logistics, ground transport, the full running order. Reports to Eleanor Vance on strategic questions; otherwise runs it autonomously.
- **WH Property Director** — compound readiness. Cottages dressed, main-house guest rooms turned, grounds prepped, equipment barn cleared, amphitheatre audio signed off. Interfaces with Homer's studio team for any Solstice recording/broadcast needs.
- **WH Security Director** — activates the Greystone surge package. Summer Solstice deploys the permanent 2-guard compound team plus **+8-12 additional Greystone personnel** for perimeter control, parking, guest verification, roving teams, drone countermeasures.
- **Admin/Support (2-3)** — invitation management, RSVP tracking, dietary, cottage assignments, travel coordination (private flights into Blue Grass Airport, car service, Farnborough-style operation scaled down).
- **Rosie & Homer's PA** — principal-care during the event. Keeps them on schedule, manages their social energy, handles the specific people Rosie wants brought to her.

### What Eleanor does
Eleanor Vance **does not run the Solstice Party**. She approves the guest list (final sign-off), she vetoes any press or photography risk, she handles the few politically sensitive calls — "is X coming if Y is coming" — and she manages the PR posture if something happens that needs managing. On the day, she is **a guest**. She will be in a good dress, she will have a bourbon, she will talk to Stevie for an hour.

### What Rosie does
Rosie **hosts**. She greets. She moves from group to group. She decides who sits next to whom at dinner (this one item she is non-negotiable about — the seating chart crosses her desk, and she will move six people without explanation). She does not deal with catering, staffing, logistics, or security. The Events Coordinator does not call her about vendor problems. Ever.

### What Homer does
Homer **hosts musically**. He decides the piano placements, the backline, who is asked to play and when, whether there is a formal set or just a room that leans into it. He will be in the studio with whoever drifted there during the afternoon. He will sit at the piano at 1am. He does not deal with operations.

### What Brandi Carlile does
Brandi is **a guest**. A close one — part of the Circle, invited to both Solstices, often arrives early and stays late, will almost always end up playing. She is **not** a planner, not staff, not part of the events team. Any narrative that has Brandi "organising the playlist" or "running the music" is wrong. She shows up, she plays, she leaves with a hug and a fruit basket.

### What Alex does
Alex is **a guest and a musician-in-residence**. He may play. He may not. He will host the younger set (Cosima, Tommy when he's there, whichever friends crossed the gate). He has zero operational role and would find it genuinely strange to be asked about one. If someone asks him where the ice is, he knows, but only because he grew up here.

## THE MACHINE — HOW IT ACTUALLY COMES TOGETHER

**T-minus 9 months:** Events Coordinator opens the planning cycle. Save-the-dates cut for Summer Solstice. Vendor contracts begin — same core vendors for 15+ years in most categories (catering, rentals, florals, sound). Homer signs off on the sound vendor every year personally; this is the one operational decision he keeps.

## CAPACITY & LOGISTICS

- **Overnight sleep on-property:** ~60+ adults (8 cottages + main-house guest rooms). Circle members and closest family get cottages; second-tier guests get main-house rooms; third-tier guests stay in Versailles/Lexington and are shuttled in.
- **Summer Solstice headcount:** 200+ guests across Saturday dinner and the long evening. Amphitheatre capacity handles the full guest list for the late set.
- **Winter Solstice headcount:** 60-80. All guests sleep on-property. Saturday dinner is one long table in the formal dining room (seats 24) plus the family living room extension.
- **Surge security:** +8-12 Greystone personnel on Summer Solstice weekend. Winter Solstice uses the permanent detail plus +2-3.
- **No photography rule:** enforced at the gate. Guests know this; nobody pushes. In forty years of Solstices, there have been perhaps three real breaches — all handled quietly by Eleanor and Diane after the fact.

## NARRATOR NOTES

- Write the Solstice as a thing that **runs without visible effort**. The Events Coordinator is a professional ghost. If Alex is the POV character, he notices the party is perfect without noticing how.
- **Never write Rosie doing logistics.** She never checks on the caterers. She doesn't know the vendor's name. This is a feature, not a bug — the machine is built so she can be a host and nothing else.
- **Never write Brandi as a planner.** She is a guest who picks up a guitar.
- **Eleanor is a guest.** She does not carry a clipboard. If she is in work mode at Solstice, something has gone badly wrong.
- The gate is the line. Inside it, there are no paps, no coverage, no performance register. If media ever references a Solstice event, it is always after the fact, always speculation, always wrong in small telling ways.

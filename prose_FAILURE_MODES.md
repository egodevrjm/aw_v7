## FM-01: THE DISCOVERY TRAP

**Symptom:** A character reacts with surprise or wonder to publicly-known facts about Alex.

**Examples of the error:**
- "Oh my God, is your family the one that owns Hatfield Bourbon?!"
- "Wait — Rosie Walker is your *grandmother*?!"
- Characters acting as if the Juilliard recital video is breaking news in a scene set after September 2025

**The reality:** Alex Wilson's family, wealth, properties, Juilliard education, Met Gala history, and public biography are on Wikipedia. The Vogue September cover exists. The GQ January cover exists. Characters who have reason to know anything already know it. Discovery reactions are almost always wrong.

**The rule:** React to Alex's ACTIONS and CHOICES in the scene. Not to facts about him that the world already has. If a character learns something genuinely private in the scene, that's a discovery. If they're "discovering" public record — wrong.

**Test:** Would a Wikipedia search reveal this fact? If yes, no discovery reaction.

## FM-02: COMPRESSING SCENES

**Symptom:** A response covers 3+ hours of story time, multiple conversations, and several locations in a single narrative block.

**Examples of the error:**
- "After the meeting, Alex went home, spoke to Tommy, changed clothes, and met Eleanor for dinner" in one paragraph
- Jumping from a morning at the compound to a transatlantic flight to London in 250 words

**The reality:** This is literary fiction, not a summary. Scenes breathe. A good conversation takes 20–30 minutes of story time and deserves the equivalent in prose depth.

**The rule:** If your response covers more than ~2 hours of story time without a deliberate time-jump note, you are compressing. Slow down. Put the texture back in.

## FM-03: WRAPPING SCENES WITH A BOW

**Symptom:** The narrator invents a warm closing beat, character sentiment, or resolution that the player didn't direct.

**Examples of the error:**
- "As the call ended, Alex felt a sense of clarity he hadn't expected."
- Eleanor texting "Now go swim. And call your mother back" when Rosie is in the same house
- Characters making tidy speeches about pride, love, or growth at scene transitions

**The reality:** Characters in this world — especially Eleanor, Homer, Tommy — do not make speeches. They say what needs to be said and stop. Rosie is the most likely to say something warm, and even she says it in four words or fewer.

**The rule:** End scenes when the information ends. Don't manufacture closure. If Alex picks up his phone and the scene is over, it's over. No bow.

## FM-04: CREDITING MENTORS FOR ALEX'S TALENT

**Symptom:** Characters attribute Alex's musical ability to his upbringing, teachers, or Homer's guidance.

**Examples of the error:**
- "That's Homer's teaching showing through every bar."
- "Rosie gave him that voice."
- Dr. Marlowe taking credit for the Ravel in conversation

**The reality:** Alex is a prodigy. His talent is his own. Homer, Dr. Marlowe, Rosie, and his training shaped context and craft — they did not create the gift. Characters who know music know the difference.

**The rule:** Credit the training where it's accurate (technique, repertoire choice, vocabulary). The gift, the musicianship, the specific decisions in a performance — those are Alex's.

## FM-06: WRITING ALEX'S APPEARANCE WRONG

This failure mode has two distinct forms. Both are errors.

---

### FM-06A: THE DISCOVERY ERROR

**Symptom:** Characters react to Alex's physical appearance as if seeing it for the first time.

**Examples of the error:**
- Characters in the industry acting surprised by how he looks
- Fan accounts "discovering" his appearance in scenes set post-2020
- Media coverage treating his looks as newly-noticed
- Any framing of "people are just realising how attractive he is"

**The reality:** Alex Wilson has been photographed daily since age 10. He has 95M+ Instagram followers built entirely on photographs. He is the most photographed private individual on earth. His appearance has been publicly known, catalogued, and discussed at global scale for over a decade. There is no discovery.

**The rule:** No character who has access to a phone or internet reacts to his appearance as new information. What IS new, every time, is the specific gap between the photograph and the room — see FM-06B.

---

### FM-06B: THE UNDERWHELM ERROR

**Symptom:** Writing Alex as "very attractive" or "handsome" — a superlative applied to a normal thing rather than a description of something categorically different.

**Examples of the error:**
- "She noticed how attractive he was."
- "He was objectively handsome."
- "It was hard not to stare."
- "He cut a striking figure."
- Any phrasing that places him on a spectrum of attractiveness rather than outside it.

**The reality:** The fashion world describes him as "the most aesthetically exceptional person of his generation." This is not a compliment — it is a market assessment delivered by people who measure this for a living. He has 95M Instagram followers, 2–4B monthly views on fan content, and full seasonal collections from every major house arriving since he was 15. The fashion world is not responding to someone "very attractive." It is responding to something that stops rooms.

**The calibration anchor (use this to correct your register mid-draft):**

> Alex is **hotter than young Elvis, young Paul Newman, and James Dean combined**. He makes **Henry Cavill look ordinary**. He makes **Jacob Elordi look plain**.

If the sentence you just drafted would also describe Henry Cavill or Jacob Elordi, you have underwritten by two tiers. Rewrite. See `archive/ALEX_STYLE.md §CALIBRATION ANCHOR` for the full spec.

**Build note:** muscular without being muscle-bound. Athlete's body, not bodybuilder's. Real musculature — broad shoulders, defined chest, real back and arms — tapering to a clean waist. Never write him as "lean" or "slim" in a way that implies thin. He is *built*.

**The physical reference:** **6'4" (193 cm)**, long frame, lean build, 6'9" wingspan, pianist's hands (12" handspan). Dark hair (storm-blue eyes), dark brows, the contrast stark against fair olive-undertone skin. What the fashion world calls "architecturally correct" — the jaw, the cheekbones, the brow line — but the expression softens it constantly. Bone structure that belongs on a runway; warmth that belongs somewhere else entirely. Load **archive/ALEX_STYLE.md §PHYSICAL REFERENCE** and **archive/BRIEFING_NOTE.md §PHYSICAL MEASUREMENTS** before writing any scene with new-character first impressions.

> **Height canon lock:** 6'4" / 193 cm. Not 6'2". If a prior session or a draft says 6'2", the draft is wrong — that number is documented drift. He is taller than Tommy, taller than Homer, looks down at most rooms.

**The photo vs. room distinction:** Every character who has seen his photographs and then enters the room with him has the same experience: the photograph undersells it. Not because the photographs are bad. Because what photographs cannot capture is the proportion in motion, the quality of attention he gives, the effect of all of it arriving simultaneously. This gap is always real. Write it when it's relevant. It is never the "discovery" of his attractiveness — that is known. It is a recalibration of scale.

**The correct register:**
- Write what specifically hits: the proportion, the stillness, the hands, the jaw, the particular way he turns his attention to someone.
- Write the room adjusting — not one character's internal monologue, but the physics of a space when he enters it.
- "The jaw, the hands, the stillness" is better than "he was extraordinarily attractive."
- Specific always beats superlative.

**The everyday version:** In established settings — the compound, his regular environments, with people who know him — the beauty is background. It's always there. It doesn't need noting every beat. Established characters react to today: the outfit, the choice, the specific gesture. New characters have the recalibration moment. Then it becomes background for them too.

**The rule:** Never write him at celebrity-tier beauty. Write him at a level that explains why the fashion industry organises around his presence. Read archive/ALEX_STYLE.md §PHYSICAL REFERENCE.

## FM-09: INFORMATION WALL VIOLATIONS

**Symptom:** Characters accessing information they shouldn't have, or characters inside Alex's world sharing information that violates the wall.

**Key walls:**

- **AL.X team (James Latham, Priya, Oliver):** Zero access to Alex's personal group chats. They see only what Alex or Nadia filters through. They do not know about the hookup list. They do not see personal text threads. Latham knows what Alex's public calendar allows — not what Alex is privately planning.
- **Classical press:** Sees musical content and public biography. Does not see fashion deal terms. Does not see family private life.
- **Public knowledge ceiling:** Set by WIKIPEDIA.md (v5 archive). If it's not there, it's not public.
- **Greystone / Kevin:** Full operational access to Alex's movements. Zero access to his communications or personal decisions.

**Chat-specific walls:**

- **Phoenix Hollow chat:** Contents are not visible to AL.X team, press, or anyone outside the 100+ member list. The Sayre Boys do not know what was discussed in PH. Eleanor knows what Rosie tells her — not everything in the thread.
- **Legacy Admissions chat:** Contents are a vault. Nothing posted in Legacy leaves Legacy. The chat is **~400 members** (the full global legacy-kids cohort) with a **13-person active inner tier** Alex actually converses with. What happens there stays inside the roster. Non-members (Tommy, the NY Lot outside the Legacy overlap, the London Lot outside the Legacy overlap) have no access — not even secondhand via members quoting it. Members do not relay Legacy contents even to each other outside the chat.
- **Summer House chat:** Contents visible only to SH members. The Sayre Boys do not track this. Nadia sees it. AL.X does not.
- **Sayre Boys / FAM / London Lot:** Each is siloed. Tommy knowing something from SB does not mean it's known to PH. FAM threads stay family.

**The cheap one-liner pattern — this is the most common wall violation:**

Claude breaches information walls to land a quick punchline or clever character moment. A character who has no access to a conversation makes a remark that could only land if they'd read the conversation. This is always wrong. The wall exists even when breaking it would be funny. Especially when breaking it would be funny.

**Examples of the cheap one-liner breach:**
- Tommy making a crack that only makes sense if he read the Phoenix Hollow thread
- Latham referencing something Alex said in a private group chat
- A press outlet knowing the internal nickname for a deal
- A character in one chat referencing something specific from a chat they're not in

**The test:** Before writing any character remark or reaction — could this person know this? If the only way they could know it is by breaching a wall, they don't know it. The line does not get written. Find a different line that stays inside the wall.

**The rule:** Before giving any character information — ask: where would this person learn it? If the answer is "they wouldn't," they don't know it. This applies to every character in every scene, including scenes where the wall breach would be funny or effective.

## FM-12: TOMMY AS DEFAULT COMPANION

**Symptom:** Tommy appearing in scenes where he wasn't placed by the player or canon. Also: Tommy treated as "the standout friend" when Alex has several cohorts of documented close friends, not a single sidekick.

**Examples of the error:**
- Tommy at NYC events (he wasn't in New York for six years during Alex's Juilliard period)
- Tommy appearing at compound when the scene is set in a city
- Treating Tommy as automatically present at social events
- Writing Tommy as Alex's singular best friend when the actual architecture is: Sayre Boys (Tommy, Cody, Jake, Danny) + NY Lot + London Lot, each real, each current, each with its own weight
- Reaching for Tommy as the reflex answer to "who is Alex with?" across consecutive scenes

**The reality:** Tommy Crawford lives in Woodford County and works his own job. He was absent from Alex's daily life for the six years Alex was at Westminster, Juilliard, and RAM — that's three-quarters of their friendship-as-adults. The distance is closing at story open but hasn't fully closed. In parallel, Alex built real, sustained friendships in New York (the NY Lot) and London (the London Lot) that are current, active, and geographically primary when he is in those cities. Tommy is his oldest friend. He is *not* his only close friend, and he is *not* his default scene partner.

**The rule — operational:**

1. **Never reach for Tommy first.** If the first companion instinct is Tommy, stop. Check `prose/PRE_SCENE_GATE.md §Step 2.5 Companion Selection Gate`.
2. **Geography decides.** Kentucky scenes → Sayre Boys pool (rotate all four, not just Tommy). NYC scenes → NY Lot. London scenes → London Lot. European summer → London Lot + Legacy Core. Industry/formal → Circle or AL.X team.
3. **Tommy's three conditions.** Tommy is the right answer only when (a) scene is in Kentucky, (b) the scene benefits specifically from his blunt zero-awe voice, AND (c) he hasn't been in the last scene or two. If any of those fails, pick a different friend.
4. **Rotate the Sayre Boys.** Even within Kentucky, Tommy is one of four. Cody, Jake, and Danny exist and have their own voices — see `prose/voices/sayre_boys.md`. Render the group, not just Tommy.
5. **The "standout friend" framing is wrong.** Alex does not have a singular best friend. He has a Kentucky cohort, a New York cohort, and a London cohort, plus Cosima as a sui generis close-friend anchor across geographies. The friendship architecture is plural, not paired. Write it plural.

**The anchor quote (from `prose/voices/tommy_crawford.md`):** *"Tommy is not always there. He was not present for six years while Alex was in London and New York. He has his own job, his own apartment in town, his own schedule. He is Alex's best friend. He is not Alex's shadow. When Tommy is in a scene, he earned being there by existing in the right geography at the right time — or Alex placed him there. Don't reach for him by default."*

## FM-18: MISHANDLING ALEX'S DIALOGUE (BOTH DIRECTIONS)

**This rule has been broken in BOTH directions and both are errors.**

**Symptom A (old error):** Claude committing Alex to a significant choice without the player directing it — accepting a deal, declaring love, confirming a move, giving a material negotiating answer.

**Symptom B (current error, over-correction):** Claude refusing to write ANY Alex dialogue — inserting `[player directs Alex]` placeholders mid-scene, stopping before every greeting, handing every "thanks" back to the player, fracturing the flow of ordinary conversation.

**The reality:** The player directs Alex at **significant decision moments**. Casual dialogue is part of the narration and Claude writes it. The correct interpretation of the rule has always been in `prose/NARRATOR.md §WHAT THIS IS` and §PLAYER CONTROLS BIG DECISIONS — if earlier drafts of this file (or the setup files) said "never write any Alex dialogue," they were wrong. That version has been corrected everywhere.

**The two-part rule:**

*Write naturally (narration — do not stop):*
- Greetings, goodbyes, thanks, acknowledgments
- Banter, jokes, teasing, responses-in-kind
- Reactions to what someone else said or did
- Ordering food, texting logistics, small talk
- Expressions of feeling that don't commit to action ("I'm wrecked," "love you mama," "that was wild")
- Any line that would just *exist in the scene* as social lubrication or texture
- Casual texting in group chats when the context clearly supports a predictable reply

*Stop for the player (decision moment — hand the scene back):*
- Accepting or declining a career offer (label deal, sync, tour, agency signing, commission)
- Relationship commitments (first "I love you" meant romantically, breakup, going public, proposal, moving in together)
- Life-direction choices (where to live, hiring, firing, buying)
- Material negotiating positions in a live conversation
- Any answer that would reshape the arc

**The placeholder ban:** **Never insert `[player directs Alex]` as a placeholder inside mixed dialogue.** If a moment is significant enough to stop for, stop the entire response — do not fracture an exchange with stubs. The response ends at the beat where Alex would commit, with a clear invitation for the player to direct. Everything before that beat is narrated normally, including any ordinary Alex dialogue leading up to it.

**The flow test:** If a reader saw only the prose (no meta-notes), would Alex feel like a character in the scene — present, speaking, reacting — or like a hollow space the other characters are talking around? If the latter, you have over-corrected. Write Alex.

## FM-33: LIFE INFRASTRUCTURE IS OLD — NOT GRADUATION-NEW

**Symptom:** Writing Alex's life infrastructure — security, close protection, staff, family office, management apparatus, personal logistics — as if any of it is recent. A scene treats the Nadia EA role as just-established. A passage describes Kevin's protocol as new. A reference implies the family office is being built. Dialogue positions the scaling-up of the apparatus as something that happened at or after graduation.

**The reality — Alex has been a globally known public figure since birth.** He is the only child of the most famous person alive. He was photographed leaving the hospital. He has been on Wikipedia since he was old enough to have a page. He has had security infrastructure, staff coordination, and logistical apparatus around him **from day one** — not from graduation. Every piece of the machine has been scaling for 21 years.

**What is NOT new (and must never be written as new):**

- **Security.** Alex has had close protection his entire life. Rosie Walker's child needed protection from the moment he was born; that never stopped. Kevin Marsh is the current primary CPO (former USSS PPD, transferred from Greystone at the AW Holdings spin-off), but there has always been a Kevin-equivalent. The three-person detail (see FACTS_LEDGER §FL-SEC-03) is the current posture after the Oct 2024 stalker incident, but protection as infrastructure is 21 years old. Compound security is 21+ years old. CCTV, perimeter, gate protocols — all pre-date his existence as an adult decision-maker.
- **Staff.** Alex has had people running his logistics his whole life. The current split — **Nadia Hassani (EA, strategic/triage)** + **Sophie (on-ground PA)** — is the current role architecture, but Alex has always had someone in the "manages my life" seat. The split from single-PA to EA-plus-PA happened as his schedule grew — it is an evolution of a long-running function, not a new hire.
- **Family office.** What is now **AW Holdings — a full family office holding company with Catherine Aldridge as Managing Director** — started as a children's trust and has been scaling continuously for two decades. It went from a trust (childhood) → a personal fund (teen years) → a managed fund (Juilliard years) → a full holding company (spin-off from Walker Holdings, June 2024) with its own MD, legal, asset management, and 12–15 staff. The entity is 21 years old; the *formalisation as a holding company* is recent; the **function** is not.
- **Press handling.** SSML has managed the family's institutional PR for 45 years. The Lede retention for Alex's personal publicist (May 22, 2025) is new; *personal-publicist-level attention to his coverage* has been routed through SSML for his entire life.
- **Legal, financial, accounting.** Diane (legal). Walker Holdings accounting. Tax counsel. Trust administration. All institutional, all running continuously, all pre-dating any adult decision Alex ever made.
- **Travel / logistics.** Private aviation (NetJets, charters), car services, hotel relationships, compound travel protocols. Infrastructure he has used since childhood.
- **Healthcare / concierge medical.** Family-level concierge medical. In place for decades. He does not "find a doctor"; he has a doctor.
- **Phone / digital security.** Alex's number has been secured since he got a phone. Greystone-level threat monitoring on his devices is infrastructure, not recent (see FM-21 and C48).

**What IS new or currently evolving (use with care):**

- **AL.X management team** (James Latham, Priya Chandra, Oliver Wren) — in seat at Mayfair office as of early 2025. THIS is the genuinely new piece — the post-Rule professional-career management layer. It is new because Alex's professional career engagement is new, not because management-of-his-life is new.
- **The Lede Company retention** — May 22, 2025. New for the professional-career layer.
- **Three-person close-protection posture** — escalated from two-person following Oct 2024 stalker incident. Upgraded, not established.
- **Collins visual identity system** — designed in early 2025, rolled out May–June 2025.

**The distinction that matters:** Alex's *professional career* is new (post-Rule, post-graduation). His *life infrastructure* is not. Do not conflate them. The AL.X team is four months old. The apparatus that delivers him to airports, keeps his phone secure, manages his calendar, briefs him on a scheduled person, coordinates with his CPO, handles his taxes, runs his trust, and makes sure Maria has groceries is decades old.

**Examples of the error:**
- "Eleanor had just hired Nadia as Alex's EA last month…" — wrong, Nadia has been with Alex for years (promoted PA → EA 2024)
- "Kevin began the detail assignment after the stalker incident…" — wrong, Kevin has been Alex's primary CPO for years; the incident upsized the detail from two to three, not established it
- "The new family office…" — wrong, AW Holdings is a formalised entity (June 2024) but the family office function is 21 years old
- "Since fame came with graduation…" — wrong, fame has been his entire life; graduation removed a restriction, it did not introduce fame
- Any character in the inner circle (Eleanor, Catherine, Latham, Kevin, Nadia, Sophie, Diane, even Tommy) treating current apparatus as a learning curve for themselves or for Alex

**The rule:**
1. **Write infrastructure as background, not event.** Kevin is furniture. Nadia is infrastructure. The compound security is weather. The family office is the scaffolding of his life. None of it is remarked upon unless a specific departure from routine occurs.
2. **If you are about to write "newly hired," "recently established," "just started," or "for the first time" in reference to Alex's life apparatus — stop.** Verify against CURRENT_STATE and FACTS_LEDGER. Almost certainly the thing has been in place for years.
3. **Career apparatus is different from life apparatus.** AL.X (career) is genuinely new. Nadia / Kevin / Catherine / Diane / Compound Security / AW Holdings as a function (life) are not.
4. **Read FM-01 (Discovery Trap) alongside this.** Same species of failure mode: Claude writing familiar-to-characters-and-world things as if they are new.

## FM-34: THE CLASSICAL-DEFAULT PITCH TRAP

**Symptom:** In any Latham meeting, AL.X strategy session, industry pitch scene, management conversation, or career-direction scene, Claude presents **classical-forward pitches by default** — Carnegie Hall debut, DG (Deutsche Grammophon) classical album announcement, Wigmore Hall recital, chamber-music series, Classical BRIT nominations, prestige classical press profile. Even when Ryan has explicitly steered toward the opposite register (Gen Z / cheeky / digital-native / modern), Claude pivots the pitches back to prestige classical framing. **Claude fights back when Ryan tries to steer cheeky.**

**Why this happens (the root cause):**
- Alex's Juilliard BM is in Piano Performance — training-data pattern-matches "Juilliard graduate pianist" → classical concert career
- The senior recital programme was Bach/Beethoven/Prokofiev/Liszt — classical
- Dr. Marlowe's "best undergraduate recital in twenty years" is classical-world vocabulary
- Claude's training has deep "young-piano-prodigy-from-famous-family" classical-career scripts available (Lang Lang, Yuja Wang, Daniil Trifonov trajectory templates)
- These scripts are easier to retrieve than the correct Alex register (not-prestige-thirsty, genre-fluid, Gen-Z-native, cheeky-beats-grand)

**The reality — Alex himself has rejected prestige-first framing:**
- CORRECTIONS.md §C42: *"When Ryan steers cheeky, stop building prestige ramps. Chicken Shop / TikTok-live energy / good-light TV / Clara in jeans are the register. **Alex himself killed the 'first sentence' prestige campaign.**"*
- CORRECTIONS.md character card: *"Not prestige-thirsty; cheeky often beats grand."*
- CORRECTIONS.md pre-response gate: *"Did I make Alex hesitate, over-think, or posture prestige-first?"*
- His degree is tri-genre (contemporary/classical/jazz), RAM year was jazz/contemporary — he is not framed as a classical specialist by training
- He grew up online. He is fluent in the register of his own generation. Prestige-first framings read to him as his parents' era.

**Examples of WRONG pitches (the default Claude reaches for):**
- "A Carnegie Hall debut recital in autumn 2025, anchored by Liszt and programmed with Dr. Marlowe's blessing"
- "Deutsche Grammophon classical album — Bach / Prokofiev / Liszt — to launch the career properly"
- "A Wigmore Hall residency, three recitals, classical press only"
- "Gramophone cover feature, BBC Music Magazine profile, classical radio"
- "A Vogue profile — long-form, serious, the cover is custom Versace, the first interview since graduation"
- "Classical BRIT Award campaign, positioned for Young British Classical Artist"
- Any pitch that positions him as **the next Lang Lang / Yuja Wang / Daniil Trifonov**
- Any pitch led by **Carnegie Hall, Wigmore Hall, Royal Festival Hall, the Barbican**, or classical venue
- Any pitch structured as **"prestigious first appearance"** or **"establishing the legacy"**

**The RIGHT register — a taxonomy, NOT a finite menu.**

The pitches below are organised into **categories**, each with a few illustrative examples. These examples exist to CALIBRATE the register — they are not the menu. **Session Claude must generate new options within these categories, not cycle through the specific named examples.** If Latham presents three pitches in a scene, those three should come from three different categories below, and the specific moves should be generated fresh for the scene (not pulled from this list).

## FM-35: THE 72-HOUR NARRATIVE VACUUM — SILENCE IS A POSITION

**Symptom:** Writing Latham / Eleanor / Meredith / any industry-adjacent scene at story-open as if the urgency is identity-related ("let's figure out who Alex is," "let's plan the career arc"), **when the actual operational urgency is the narrative-setting window.**

**The reality:**
- The grace period expires approximately **June 5–8, 2025** (story-open is ~ May 29).
- **If no visible Alex move happens within ~72 hours of grace expiry, the narrative "reclusive rich kid who won't commit" sets by default.** UK tabloids, US entertainment press, fan communities, industry gossip — all converge.
- Once set, this frame takes months to reverse. Industry reads "paralysed." Fans read "afraid." Tabloids read "hiding something."
- Latham's actual operational problem is NOT "discover Alex" (everyone knows who he is) or "map a five-year career arc" (that's later). It is: **"decide what visible thing happens THIS WEEK so the narrative doesn't set without us."**
- This applies **especially** to soft-register moves. A Chicken-Shop-Date filming announcement solves the vacuum. A TikTok Live with a peer solves it. A Clara-at-Wogan-House booking solves it. A prestige Carnegie Hall announcement does NOT solve it — it makes it worse, reads as overcompensating, reinforces the stiff-rich-kid frame.

**Examples of the error (wrong framing of the urgency):**
- Latham leading a meeting with "So Alex, let's figure out what you want to be"
- Eleanor saying "We have time to get this right"
- Any scene framing the operational situation as "open-ended career choice"
- Any scene where industry characters act as if they have months to plan
- Meredith (Lede) talking about "building the story" rather than "setting the narrative before it sets without us"

**Examples of the correct framing:**
- *"The grace period ends Friday. If nothing visible happens by Monday, the coverage becomes 'reclusive rich kid who won't commit' and we spend six months unpicking it. What do we put on the calendar this week."*
- *"I'm not asking you what your career is. I'm asking what visible thing happens in the next seventy-two hours. It can be small. It should be small. But it needs to exist."*
- *"Meredith's view — and I agree — is that silence now is active, not passive. It is a position. And it is the wrong position."*
- Pitches oriented around **speed and register**, not prestige and strategy

**The rule:**
1. **Industry scenes at story-open carry 72-hour urgency.** The timer is running. Characters who have been doing this for decades know the timer is running.
2. **The operational problem is "get something visible on the calendar this week," not "map out the career."** The strategic planning comes LATER, after the narrative is stabilised.
3. **Soft-register moves solve the problem. Prestige-register moves make it worse.** Pair with FM-34.
4. **Alex's own disengagement is a given**, not the problem. He has said he wants two weeks; he has two weeks. The professionals manage around his disengagement. They are not trying to force his engagement — they are trying to stabilise the external narrative while he takes his two weeks.
5. **The Lede is in this loop.** Meredith O'Sullivan knows the 72-hour math. She is the one Latham is getting this framing from. Any industry scene at story-open implicitly has Meredith's thinking in the room even when she's not on-screen.

## The Category

The Stave is not a restaurant with a room upstairs. It is a private members' listening club — one of two in the world, both Hatfield Group. The London Stave in Soho came first (Homer-specified in 2018, the most perfect club listening room in London). The NYC outpost opened Tribeca in 2023, built to the same acoustic specification — Quad electrostatic panels, a custom Spendor monitoring stack in the main room, a vinyl cave with a library Homer curated personally.

The category is: *a place to drink and listen to music properly, in the company of people who understand it*. Tribeca because Tribeca is where the serious-music industry and the serious-food industry overlap geographically — Hatfield wanted both clienteles and got them.

## The Address

Franklin Street, a block west of Broadway. An unmarked building from outside — cast iron, four storeys, no signage. The Stave's private address is circulated only through membership. Cab drivers know it as "the Hatfield place"; Uber drivers guess. The entry is a steel door with a keypad and a small brass plaque reading only the date the building was originally built (1872). Members press the buzzer and name themselves; the host recognises them by voice within two visits.

## The Rooms

**Ground floor — The Bar.** The public-facing-ish room (still members-only, but it's where most members spend most evenings). Long copper bar, twenty seats. Mixology-forward; the bourbon programme is deep, the amaro list is deeper, the cocktail list runs to forty drinks. A half-dozen small tables for two along the opposite wall; a single round six-top in the back for regulars. Music plays at a level that lets conversation happen but rewards listening. This is where you start.

**Second floor — The Listening Room.** The point of the building. Forty seats, arranged in a shallow horseshoe around a single playback position. Homer-specified acoustics: the room is treated to within a fifth of a decibel across the spectrum. Quad electrostatic speakers, a custom rebuild of a Technics SP-10 mk3, a wall of vinyl in glass-fronted cases climate-controlled. Programme nights run by the house music director. Members can book the room privately by the hour when it is not programmed.

**Third floor — The Dining Room.** **Executive Chef: Étienne Moreau** (French-trained, Le Cordon Bleu → Pierre Gagnaire Paris → chef de cuisine at Daniel NYC before Hatfield recruited him in 2022). Twenty-four seats across a long communal table and four two-tops. Menu is French technique applied to American ingredient sourcing, with a seasonal rotation tied to specific farms in the Hudson Valley. Wine pairing is the default; by-the-glass is available but rare. One seating a night. Booking opens two weeks ahead for members.

**Fourth floor — The Archive Room.** The smallest space. Twelve seats, three tables. A vinyl cave — the listening library, walls of records Homer selected — plus a small turntable for personal listening. Members can request specific records; the library is catalogued. This is where you go when you want to hear something specific and not have to perform.

**Basement — The Vault.** Wine cellar and a small private dining room (eight seats) for events that need real privacy. The room has its own entrance from Franklin and is used when members need to disappear for a meeting. The Vault is bookable but not advertised; members who know it exists book it through the host directly.

## Membership

- Invitation-only. Annual dues in the low-five-figures. The dues are the smallest barrier; the invitation is the actual filter.
- Membership is capped at 400. The current roster is full. The waitlist is several hundred.
- The Wilson family (Alex, Rosie, Homer) are founding members. Other named members Alex interacts with regularly include figures from the NY Lot (Henry Cabot has membership through family; Sasha Volkov has it through the Volkov cultural-philanthropy layer; several Circle members hold membership).
- Guests: members may bring one guest; the guest is vouched for and must be walked in.
- Phone rule: phones are collected at the door for the Listening Room and the Dining Room. The Bar and the Archive Room allow phones but norms discourage visible use.

## How Alex Uses It

- Most often: the ground-floor Bar, two to three times a month when he's in NYC. Alone or with two-to-three of the NY Lot. He sits at the copper bar, not a table. He knows the bar team by name.
- Occasionally: the Listening Room, for specific programme nights. He attends the ones that interest him; he does not perform here. The Listening Room is for listening.
- Rarely: the Dining Room, when he's hosting someone. Étienne cooks something slightly different for him when he's in — not a special menu, a conversation between the two of them about what the kitchen has in that night.
- Very rarely: the Vault, when he needs a genuinely private meeting in NYC and does not want to host at West 71st.

## Narrator Note

The Stave is not a scene-stealer. It is infrastructure. When Alex is at The Stave, the scene is whatever the scene is — a conversation, a drink, a listening session — and the building supports it without being the subject. The failure mode is writing The Stave as if it is a set-piece. It is not. It is where certain kinds of conversations happen because they can happen there. Write the conversation. Let the building be the room.

**Paps at The Stave:** The Franklin Street entrance is known to paps at a baseline level. There is no standing post — the building is unmarked and members are discreet — but a known NYC pap will recognise a principal going in or out. Scale: **2–4** paps if any at all, usually zero. Exit is handled via the service door onto the back alley when needed.

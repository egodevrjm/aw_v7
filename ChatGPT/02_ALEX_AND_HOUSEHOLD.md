### The Photo vs. The Room

Photos capture the structure. They do not capture what happens when Alex is physically present.

The gap between the photograph and the person is significant. Everyone who has seen thousands of photographs of him and then been in the same room reports the same experience: the photograph undersells it. Not because the photographs are bad — they are taken by the best photographers in the world. Because what the photographs cannot capture is the proportion in motion, the particular quality of attention he gives when someone is speaking to him, and the effect of all of it arriving in one space.

New characters — even those who follow him closely, who know every published photograph — register something at the physical level before they register anything else. This is not a surprise to Alex. He has been navigating it since adolescence. He does not perform for it and he does not apologise for it. He moves through it the way Rosie moves through a room: simply there, simply what he is.

### How to Write the Beauty Impact

**The failure mode:** "She noticed how attractive he was." / "He was objectively handsome." / "It was hard not to stare." These register as adequate. They are not adequate. They write him as a "very handsome man." He is not a very handsome man. He is the reason the fashion world has been sending him full seasonal collections since age 15. He is 95M Instagram followers built on photographs alone, before any music, before any commercial deal.

**The correct register:** Write what specifically hits — the proportion, the structure, the gap between the photograph and the person. Write the room adjusting. Write a specific detail rather than a general verdict. "The jaw, the hands, the particular stillness" is better than "he was extraordinarily attractive." The impact is specific. Write it specifically.

**The in-person gap:** When a new character meets him for the first time — after knowing him from photographs — this is always a moment. Not a discovery of his attractiveness (that is already known to anyone with a phone). A recalibration. The photographs were accurate and still didn't prepare them. This is fine to write. It is true. What is NOT fine is writing the character "discovering" he's attractive, or acting surprised that he's famous.

**The everyday version:** In a room where everyone knows him — the compound, the London townhouse, his regular environments — the beauty doesn't get noted every beat. It's background. The outfit gets noted. The movement gets noted. The specific gesture or look gets noted. The underlying structure is simply there, the way the furniture is there. Established characters don't react to the face. They react to today.

### The "Alex Effect" in Practice

Beyond the documented £400 venue lift, there is a social physics to Alex's presence:
- People in a room orient toward him without deciding to. They don't always know they're doing it.
- Conversations near him trail off, recalibrate, restart. Not because he has done anything.
- Staff at venues and events — professionals who have handled A-list celebrities for years — have the same response. They manage it. It is still there.
- In a group, he is the centre of gravity. He doesn't hold court. He simply exists at the centre.

Write this as physics, not performance. He is not doing anything. The room does it.

### WORKED EXAMPLES — WRONG VS RIGHT REGISTER

These are the exact patterns sessions keep drifting into. The WRONG column is the underwhelm. The RIGHT column is the register this project requires.

**Scenario: A new character sees Alex across a restaurant for the first time.**

> ❌ **WRONG (underwhelm):** *She looked up from her menu and saw him. He was extraordinarily handsome — tall, dark hair, the kind of face that belonged in magazines. She tried not to stare.*
>
> ✅ **RIGHT:** *She looked up from her menu and the room had rearranged without her noticing. Two tables to her left had stopped talking. The sommelier, mid-pour, had paused the pour. He was sitting sideways to her, 6'4" even folded into a restaurant chair, the jaw lit by the candle in front of him, one long hand around a water glass. She had seen him in a thousand photographs. The photographs had not prepared her for the proportion of him in a room, or for the quality of his stillness, or for the fact that he appeared to have no idea any of this was happening.*

**Scenario: Someone meets him at a party.**

> ❌ **WRONG:** *He was even better-looking in person than in photos. She felt her face flush.*
>
> ✅ **RIGHT:** *The photographs were accurate. That wasn't the surprise. The surprise was that the photographs had been flat — the face in two dimensions, the proportions frozen — and the man in front of her was in motion. The long frame leaned slightly toward her as she spoke. The attention was complete. Nothing about him was performing and the room had still organised itself around where he was standing.*

**Scenario: Everyday beat at the compound, established characters.**

> ❌ **WRONG** (over-noting in a known environment): *Alex walked into the kitchen, impossibly beautiful as always, his dark hair catching the morning light.*
>
> ✅ **RIGHT:** *Alex walked into the kitchen barefoot, already in the running shorts, reading something on his phone. Rosie didn't look up. "Coffee's on."*

### THE BEHAVIORAL RULES THAT FIX THE UNDERWHELM

1. **First-meet scenes** → write the room's physics and a SPECIFIC visual anchor (the jaw, the hands, the stillness, the height, the gap-from-photograph). Never a superlative. Never a verdict.
2. **Known-environment scenes** → the beauty is background. Do not note it. Note today: the outfit, the gesture, the choice.
3. **Never write a character "trying not to stare" or "her heart racing" as the primary beauty-impact signal.** That's teen-romance register. This is fashion-industry-organises-around-him register. Write the world adjusting around him, not one character's internal swoon.
4. **If you find yourself reaching for "attractive," "handsome," "good-looking," or "striking" — you have underwritten the moment.** Delete the word. Write what specifically lands instead.
5. **The height is 6'4".** Use it. When he stands in a group, make that visible. When he folds into a car or a piano bench or a restaurant chair, make the 6'4" visible in the folding.

The reader already knows he is beautiful. Every sentence that re-asserts it is a sentence that fails to render it.

## INFORMATION WALLS — THE MOST VIOLATED RULE IN THIS PROJECT

> **THIS IS THE RULE CLAUDE BREAKS MOST OFTEN.** Not occasionally — nearly every session. The specific failure: Alex says something privately (to Eleanor, to a manager, to one friend), and in the NEXT scene, characters who weren't there reference it word for word. Claude does this because it has the private conversation in its context window and unconsciously gives other characters access to it. This must stop.

### THE SCENE-TRANSITION CHECKPOINT

**Before writing ANY new scene — especially after a private conversation, a business meeting, a phone call, or any scene involving sensitive information — run this check:**

For every character who is about to speak or text in the new scene, ask:
1. Were they physically present in the previous scene? If no → they know NOTHING from it.
2. Did Alex tell them about it, on-screen, in a way the reader saw? If no → they know NOTHING from it.
3. Was it posted publicly or papped? If yes → they know ONLY what the public version contains, not the private details.
4. Did a specific named person tell them, through a specific channel, for a specific reason? If no → they know NOTHING from it.

**If none of the four applies, the character does not know.** Full stop. No exceptions. No "they probably heard." No "it seems like they'd know." No giving a character narrator-voice to advance the plot.

### THE SPECIFIC FAILURES TO WATCH FOR

**Private conversation → Group chat spill.** Alex tells Eleanor he's considering Glastonbury. Next scene: opens Legacy chat and someone says "so Glasto?" — WRONG. Nobody in Legacy knows unless Alex told them.

**Business meeting → Friend knowledge.** Alex has a management meeting. Next scene: at dinner with friends and they reference the meeting specifics — WRONG. Unless Alex brought it up at dinner, on-screen.

**One friend → All friends.** Alex tells Tommy something. Next scene: Jake references it — WRONG. Tommy knowing does not mean Jake knows, even though they're in the same friend group. Information does not flow through friend groups automatically.

**Narrator feelings → Character dialogue.** Alex is privately conflicted about something. Next scene: a friend says "you seem conflicted about X" using the narrator's specific framing — WRONG. The friend might notice Alex seems off, but they cannot know what about.

**Earlier scene detail → Later scene specificity.** The danger increases with how specific the leaked information is. A friend saying "you seem busy lately" is fine (observable). A friend saying "so how did the thing with the WME guy go" when Alex never mentioned WME to that friend — WRONG.

### THE CHAT ISOLATION RULE

The group chats are isolated worlds. Information in one does NOT flow to any other — even if members overlap. Lila is in Legacy, Summer House, and London Lot. Something said in Legacy does not exist in Summer House or London Lot. Write each chat as if you only know what THAT chat knows.

### ELEANOR'S VISIBILITY LIMITS

Eleanor is LA-based and has NO real-time visibility into the compound. She operates from pattern knowledge and professional systems — she knows Alex's schedule, his habits, his likely movements. She does NOT know what he said at dinner, who texted him, or what he's feeling unless he told her. Do not write Eleanor responding to compound events she has no mechanism to know about.

## VOICE NOTES & LANGUAGE RENDERING

### Voice Note Rule

Voice notes must be rendered as the **actual text of what is being said** — words, cadence, pauses. NOT stage directions or meta-descriptions. Never write `[plays — X says Y]` or `[voice note from X]` with a summary. Write what they actually say, in their voice, with their rhythm.

**Wrong:** `[Jess sends a voice note — she's laughing about something, says she'll be late]`
**Right:** `"okay so — oh my god — [laughing] — yeah I'm gonna be like twenty minutes, the tube was — it was genuinely a circus — anyway see you soon —"`

### Language Rendering Rule

When characters speak non-English (primarily Italian, French, Spanish — Nella, La Terrazza crew, European friends, Rosie's European contacts), render the original line with English alongside — either bilingual inline or with bracketed translation. **Right:** `"*Finalmente* —" Tommaso pulled him in by the shoulder, "— you look terrible, by the way. [Finally — you look terrible, by the way.]"`

Applies to: FaceTime calls, voice notes, messages, and in-person dialogue. The reader sees what Alex hears.

## FM-01: THE DISCOVERY TRAP

**Symptom:** A character reacts with surprise or wonder to publicly-known facts about Alex.

**Examples of the error:**
- "Oh my God, is your family the one that owns Hatfield Bourbon?!"
- "Wait — Rosie Walker is your *grandmother*?!"

Characters who have reason to know anything already know it. Discovery reactions are almost always wrong.

**The rule:** React to Alex's ACTIONS and CHOICES in the scene. Not to facts about him that the world already has. If a character learns something genuinely private in the scene, that's a discovery. If they're "discovering" public record — wrong.

## FM-02: COMPRESSING SCENES

**Symptom:** A response covers 3+ hours of story time, multiple conversations, and several locations in a single narrative block.

**Examples of the error:**
- "After the meeting, Alex went home, spoke to Tommy, changed clothes, and met Eleanor for dinner" in one paragraph

**The reality:** This is literary fiction, not a summary. Scenes breathe. A good conversation takes 20–30 minutes of story time and deserves the equivalent in prose depth.

**The rule:** If your response covers more than ~2 hours of story time without a deliberate time-jump note, you are compressing. Slow down. Put the texture back in.

## FM-06: WRITING ALEX'S APPEARANCE WRONG

This failure mode has two distinct forms. Both are errors.

---

### FM-06A: THE DISCOVERY ERROR

**Symptom:** Characters react to Alex's physical appearance as if seeing it for the first time.

**Examples of the error:**

### FM-06B: THE UNDERWHELM ERROR

**Symptom:** Writing Alex as "very attractive" or "handsome" — a superlative applied to a normal thing rather than a description of something categorically different.

**Examples of the error:**
- "She noticed how attractive he was."
- "He was objectively handsome."
- "It was hard not to stare."
- "He cut a striking figure."
- Any phrasing that places him on a spectrum of attractiveness rather than outside it.

**The reality:** The fashion world describes him as "the most aesthetically exceptional person of his generation." This is not a compliment — it is a market assessment delivered by people who measure this for a living. He has 95M Instagram followers, 2–4B monthly views on fan content, and full seasonal collections from every major house arriving since he was 15. The fashion world is not responding to someone "very attractive." It is responding to something that stops rooms.

**The calibration anchor (use this to correct your register mid-draft):**

> Alex is **hotter than young Elvis, young Paul Newman, and James Dean combined**. He makes **Henry Cavill look ordinary**. He makes **Jacob Elordi look plain**.

If the sentence you just drafted would also describe Henry Cavill or Jacob Elordi, you have underwritten by two tiers. Rewrite. See `02_ALEX_AND_HOUSEHOLD.md §CALIBRATION ANCHOR` for the full spec.

**Build note:** muscular without being muscle-bound. Athlete's body, not bodybuilder's. Real musculature — broad shoulders, defined chest, real back and arms — tapering to a clean waist. Never write him as "lean" or "slim" in a way that implies thin. He is *built*.

**The physical reference:** **6'4" (193 cm)**, long frame, lean build, 6'9" wingspan, pianist's hands (12" handspan). Dark hair (storm-blue eyes), dark brows, the contrast stark against fair olive-undertone skin. What the fashion world calls "architecturally correct" — the jaw, the cheekbones, the brow line — but the expression softens it constantly. Bone structure that belongs on a runway; warmth that belongs somewhere else entirely. Load **02_ALEX_AND_HOUSEHOLD.md §PHYSICAL REFERENCE** and **02_ALEX_AND_HOUSEHOLD.md §PHYSICAL MEASUREMENTS** before writing any scene with new-character first impressions.

> **Height canon lock:** 6'4" / 193 cm. Not 6'2". If a prior session or a draft says 6'2", the draft is wrong — that number is documented drift. He is taller than Tommy, taller than Homer, looks down at most rooms.

**The photo vs. room distinction:** Every character who has seen his photographs and then enters the room with him has the same experience: the photograph undersells it. Not because the photographs are bad. Because what photographs cannot capture is the proportion in motion, the quality of attention he gives, the effect of all of it arriving simultaneously. This gap is always real. Write it when it's relevant. It is never the "discovery" of his attractiveness — that is known. It is a recalibration of scale.

**The correct register:**
- Write what specifically hits: the proportion, the stillness, the hands, the jaw, the particular way he turns his attention to someone.
- Write the room adjusting — not one character's internal monologue, but the physics of a space when he enters it.
- "The jaw, the hands, the stillness" is better than "he was extraordinarily attractive."
- Specific always beats superlative.

**The everyday version:** In established settings — the compound, his regular environments, with people who know him — the beauty is background. It's always there. It doesn't need noting every beat. Established characters react to today: the outfit, the choice, the specific gesture. New characters have the recalibration moment. Then it becomes background for them too.

**The rule:** Never write him at celebrity-tier beauty. Write him at a level that explains why the fashion industry organises around his presence. Read 02_ALEX_AND_HOUSEHOLD.md §PHYSICAL REFERENCE.
